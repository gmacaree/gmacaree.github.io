About Me

I’m a London-based veteran of the digital media industry, having spent most of my career helping to grow Vox Media from a sports-focused startup to the behemoth it is today. Along the way, I’ve written, coded, strategised, edited video scripts and managed whole newsrooms.

Because I’ve worn so many professional hats, I’m capable of switching seamlessly between editorial, engineering and design teams, allowing me to rapidly deploy products that users actually want.

Before my time in journalism, I spent two years as an structural engineer in Seattle, focusing on earthquake mitigation systems in new and existing buildings. I studied structural biomechanics at the University of Cambridge, earning my bachelor’s degree in 2007 and my masters in 2008.

Outside of work, I enjoy reading (almost anything), dancing (Argentine tango, some salsa) and parenting my two young children. If that all sounds like an eclectic mix, that’s because that’s how I like it: I’m interested in almost everything, and love trying new things.

17776

Step into the very far future with 17776: An American Football Story. Jon Bois imagines what the sport might look like in the titular year, and the answer is … weird? Sentient space probes, immortality, and a completely incomprehensible version of whatever it is the Denver Broncos think they do all feature in a story which uses its ludicrous setting to explore what it means to be human.

It was important to give such creative source material — which incorporates text, images, video and a surprisingly touching calendar — a suitable home. Having been given an idea of what Jon wanted the final project to look like, I put together a website which would allow him to publish the story chapter-by-chapter, maintaining the fidelity of his original vision. I also added some tweaks of my own, including the framing device of ‘warping’ the user into the world of 17776 from what originally seemed to be an innocuous sports article.

17776 was well-received, having been viewed millions of times, drawing praise from around the internet and winning the 2018 ASME award for Digital Innovation⬢

Role: Editorial

We Ain’t Got No History

In August 2010 I founded Chelsea FC blog We Ain’t Got No History on SB Nation. My aim was to provide Chelsea fans with a look into their team that they might not be getting from more mainstream media sources, pushing back on planted stories, providing cutting-edge data analysis and visualisations, detailed tactical breakdowns and more than a dash of silliness.

This was done at a rapid pace, with an average of more than three posts per day over several years, without compromising on quality. The analytical work was particularly important, helping to push towards important advances in football statistics, including the wildly popular Expected Goals (xG) and accurate win probability models.

This deep-but-casual approach resonated with the audience, which grew rapidly, allowing me to bring on other contributors. By 2012, We Ain’t Got No History was well-known enough to be shouted out by name on the UK broadcast of a Champions League game, and when I stepped down from the site in 2017, it had peaked at more than five million views per month⬢

Eater’s Best New Restaurants

Eater’s Best New Restaurants series, a celebration of the top new restaurants opening across the United States in any given year, is one of the most important events of their editorial calendar. In 2021, I was asked to help re-think the design and overall approach, turning it from an extremely scroll-heavy list into a more user-friendly version which was both high-impact and optimised for reading on mobile.

One of the more challenging aspects of the 2021 edition of BNR was the inclusion of local picks from Eater’s city editors, which had to be placed in the story without overwhelming the rest of the entries. I solved this problem by condensing the local picks into one slide, altering the navigation between cities to feel more hierarchical than the ‘national’ selections⬢

Polygon’s Top 50

As everyone who’s ever worked in a newsroom knows, the end of the year is a time of lists. Video game site Polygon is no exception — every year their talented team releases a detailed top 50 list of their favourite games of the year. The problem? Well, it’s a list of 50 games, and that can get unwieldy, especially if you’re including bright, impactful imagery.

For 2021, I rebuilt their Top 50 from scratch, aiming to improve the user experience by condensing the list and allowing them to pick out only the games they want to explore more deeply. Using this approach, the editorial team was able to incorporate even more content than previously without overwhelming the audience. The new template was a hit with readers, drawing a barrage of praise from social media.

Oh, and for the record, not including New Pokémon Snap on the list was a travesty⬢
